SimpleHealth is a technology-focused healthcare company on a mission to be the most accessible and affordable reproductive healthcare provider in the country.

I joined the company as their first designer with a mission to build a high-performing design function in a hypergrowth environment. My vision for the team was unusual: a centralized, service oriented design function that supported every aspect of the company, from marketing and operations to HR and product development. This unique approach was a key factor to the success of the company as it grew from 40 employees to over 200.

Key outcomes:

  • Built design team from 0 → 6 designers
  • Supported 20× revenue growth in 2 years
  • Led projects across brand, marketing, product design, data science, operations, and research
  • Redesigned and rebuilt internal-facing applications enabling a 5✕ increase in daily new patient acquisition.
  • Supported $20MM in annual ad spend with 100% in-house creative direction and execution.

Building the team

From the beginning, my goal was to build a team that was ready for anything.

At most companies, design is decentralized. Designers are organized into functional groups like marketing or product; there's no community of practice across the board, and career paths are different for each function.

My vision for the design team was a centralized one: all the designers would be on the same team. My hypothesis is that this would result in a much stronger community of practice and a more consistent user experience.

Building the team in this way allowed me to attract great design talent, increase collaboration, deepen our community of practice, and create a stronger sense of belonging.

Designing our service

Related Essay: The service-based design org

Another early win for the design team was positioning ourselves as a service rather than a fully user-facing team. This paradigm was born from the insight that design is, in some way or another, part of everything that the business does.

Applying service design methodology — like service blueprints, SLAs, and service design principles — we were able to support rapid growth across the business with a relatively small design team.

Showing results

Over the two years I led the design team at SimpleHealth, the design team was instrumental in a number of high-impact projects.

Increasing opt-in rates for new products

In March of 2021, SimpleHealth launched 5 over-the-counter and prescription health products and offered them as add-ons to our patients' birth control subscriptions.

The initial scope of the project was strictly MVP. After releasing the new products, we saw a low conversion rate on promotional screens; after a long medical questionnaire, users were quickly tapping through to complete their subscription.

The challenge was a common one: how do we iterate on the user experience to improve the opt-in rate for these products? In this case, we chose to do extensive research on competitors and comparable products to determine the best approach.

In the end, with the help of freelance research Rori Spivey, we decided to add elements of personalization throughout the intial medical questionnaire. By referring back to these choices when presenting recommendations, we were able to increase conversion rates by over 2× (3% → 7%).

Improving clarity and conversion during checkout

With any online commerce experience, the checkout interface is one of the easiest to run experiments on. Since it's at the bottom of the funnel, changes to these elements have a direct and measurable impact on conversion and customer satisfaction.

In SimpleHealth's case, the importance of these elements is compounded by the complex pricing model of the product; customers can be checking out with a combination of one-off and subscription products, and paying with a combination of credit card and health insurance coverage.
